# Example 4 — Recent Graduate

**Scenario (synthetic).** Recent Australian graduate, Bachelor of Information Technology finished in late 2025, applying for a graduate / junior Data Analyst role in Sydney. Work history is thin — part-time retail through university, one short internship, and a capstone project. The current resume leads with the retail job and buries the relevant work, so it reads as "retail worker", not "graduate analyst".

---

## INPUT

**Target role:** Graduate / Junior Data Analyst (Sydney, NSW)

**Job ad (extract):** "Support the analytics team with data cleaning, SQL queries, and dashboards. Required: SQL, Excel, some Python or R, clear communication, willingness to learn. Internship or project experience welcome."

**Current resume (extract, as supplied):**

> JESS PATEL
>
> Experience:
> Sales Assistant, Retail Co — 2021–2025
> - Served customers and operated the register
> - Restocked shelves
>
> Education:
> Bachelor of Information Technology, 2025
>
> Skills: Teamwork, Microsoft Office

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. ATS Readiness Score: 4/10
Your resume scores 4/10 — it's tidy, but it leads with retail and hides the analyst-relevant work. For a graduate role the order is the problem: a recruiter sees "Sales Assistant" first and stops reading. Main issues below.

### 2. Top 5 issues found
- ❌ Order. The retail job is first; your degree, capstone project, and internship — the relevant evidence — are last or missing. For a recent grad, lead with education and projects.
- ❌ No project or internship detail. A capstone and an internship are real, relevant experience — they belong on the page with specifics, tools, and outcomes.
- ❌ Missing the JD's keywords entirely: SQL, Excel, Python/R, dashboards, data cleaning. If you used these in study or the internship, name them.
- ❌ "Teamwork, Microsoft Office" is a weak skills line. Replace with the actual technical stack the role scans for.
- ❌ Retail isn't worthless — but it should be reframed for transferable skills (reliability, customer communication, working under pressure) and moved below the relevant content.

### 4. Keyword analysis (vs the job ad)
Top JD keywords: SQL, Excel, Python, R, dashboards, data cleaning, analytics, communication.
- Present after the rewrite: SQL, Excel, Python, dashboards, data cleaning, analytics, communication.
- Still missing: R. Action: only add R if you've actually used it. If you haven't, leave it out — the JD lists "some Python **or** R", and your Python covers it. Don't list a language you can't talk about in an interview.

### 5. AU-specific notes
- Length: one to two pages is fine for a graduate; don't pad. Quality of the project detail beats volume.
- Dates: "2021 – 2025"; spelling Australian (analyse, organise, program).
- No photo, no DOB. A recent grad doesn't need an objective line either — the summary does that job.
- Australian context: name the AU university and that the internship was AU-based; local study and experience are a genuine plus for a grad role here.

### 6. Cover letter draft (250-350 words)
Dear Hiring Manager,

I'm applying for the Graduate Data Analyst role on your team in Sydney. I finished my Bachelor of Information Technology in 2025, and the work you've described — data cleaning, SQL queries, and dashboards — is exactly what I've been doing in my capstone project and a recent internship.

For my capstone I cleaned and joined three messy datasets in SQL and built an interactive dashboard that took a client's manual report from about three hours down to under ten minutes. During an eight-week data internship I wrote SQL queries against a production database for weekly reporting and automated a recurring Excel clean-up in Python, which saved the team around an hour a week. Those are small wins, but they're the real shape of the work, and I enjoyed it enough to want to do it full-time.

I worked part-time in retail right through my degree, which taught me to manage my time, stay reliable under pressure, and explain things clearly to people who aren't technical — all of which I expect to use in an analytics team that works with the rest of the business.

I know I'm early in my career and I'm genuinely keen to learn. I'd welcome the chance to talk about how I could support your analytics team and grow into the role. Would you have 20 minutes in the coming weeks?

Kind regards,
Jess Patel
