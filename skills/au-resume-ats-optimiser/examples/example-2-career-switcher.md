# Example 2 — Career Switcher

**Scenario (synthetic).** Australian citizen, eight years as a secondary school teacher (Head of Department for the last three), now switching out of the classroom into a corporate Learning & Development Coordinator role in Melbourne. No corporate experience on paper. The current resume is written entirely in education language and reads as "teacher applying for a teaching job", which makes a recruiter screen it out for a corporate role.

---

## INPUT

**Target role:** Learning & Development Coordinator (Melbourne, VIC) — financial services

**Job ad (extract):** "Coordinate the L&D calendar, design and facilitate workshops, manage our LMS, partner with stakeholders to scope training needs, and report on completion and engagement. Required: strong facilitation, content design, stakeholder management, comfort with data/reporting. LMS experience desirable."

**Current resume (extract, as supplied):**

> SARAH NGUYEN
> Secondary Teacher
>
> Objective: Dedicated teacher seeking a new challenge.
>
> Experience:
> Head of Humanities, Northside Secondary College — 2021–present
> - Taught Years 7–12 Humanities
> - Wrote lesson plans and assessments
> - Ran the faculty and mentored new teachers

> Bachelor of Education, 2016

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. ATS Readiness Score: 5/10
Your resume scores 5/10 — clean and parsable, but it's pitched at a teaching role, not an L&D one. The ATS and the recruiter both read "teacher", not "training professional". Main issues below.

### 2. Top 5 issues found
- ❌ The objective ("seeking a new challenge") says nothing and dates the resume. Replace with a summary that names the L&D target and your transferable evidence.
- ❌ Everything is in education language ("lesson plans", "faculty", "Years 7–12"). Translate it into the corporate L&D equivalents the ATS scans for: content design, facilitation, stakeholder management, capability uplift.
- ❌ No quantified outcomes. "Mentored new teachers" → how many, with what result.
- ❌ Missing the JD's keywords: LMS, L&D calendar, reporting, workshop design. Your real work maps onto all of these — make the mapping explicit.
- ❌ The career switch isn't framed. A recruiter shouldn't have to guess why a teacher is applying — say it in one confident line.

### 4. Keyword analysis (vs the job ad)
Top JD keywords: L&D, facilitation, content design, workshop, LMS, stakeholder management, training needs, reporting, completion, engagement.
- Present after the rewrite: facilitation, content design, workshop, LMS, stakeholder management, training needs, reporting, completion, engagement.
- Still missing: explicit "L&D calendar". Action: add one line under your most recent role — "Planned and ran a yearly teaching and assessment calendar across the department" — which is a literal L&D-calendar equivalent; name it that way.

### 5. AU-specific notes
- Dates: use "2021 – present" or "February 2021 – present", not US formats.
- Length: two pages is fine here; use the space to make the translation from teaching to L&D explicit.
- Spelling: Australian throughout (organise, specialise, program, centre).
- Don't apologise for the switch. A career change framed as deliberate reads as confident; an unexplained one reads as a fallback.

### 6. Cover letter draft (250-350 words)
Dear Hiring Manager,

I'm applying for the Learning & Development Coordinator role on your team in Melbourne. After eight years in secondary education — the last three leading a department — I'm moving into corporate L&D, and your role is exactly the bridge I'm looking for: facilitation, content design, and stakeholder partnering are what I already do every day.

Running a Humanities department meant owning a full calendar of learning, designing and delivering 200+ hours of workshops a year, and adapting content for very different audiences in the room. I tracked progress and reported outcomes to leadership each term, and I onboarded and mentored four early-career staff, all of whom we retained. Swap "students" for "employees" and "term" for "quarter" and that is the L&D loop your advertisement describes: scope the need, design the content, facilitate, and report on engagement and completion.

I'm making this move deliberately. The part of teaching I love most is building capability in others and the systems that support it — which is the whole of an L&D role and only a part of a teaching one. I pick up new tools quickly, including LMS platforms, and I'm comfortable with the data and reporting side.

I'd welcome a short conversation about how my facilitation and content-design experience could support your training calendar. Could we find 20 minutes in the next week or two?

Kind regards,
Sarah Nguyen
