# Example 3 — Government APS Role with Selection Criteria

**Scenario (synthetic).** Australian citizen applying for an APS6 Project Officer role in a Commonwealth agency. APS applications are different from private-sector ones: alongside a resume, they usually require responses to **Key Selection Criteria (KSC)**, and the strongest responses are written in **STAR** (Situation, Task, Action, Result). The applicant has the experience but has written a generic private-sector resume and hasn't addressed the criteria at all.

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. ATS Readiness Score: 5/10
Your resume scores 5/10 — but for an APS role the resume is only half the job. There are no responses to the selection criteria, and that's usually what determines whether you make the shortlist. Main issues below.

### 2. Top 5 issues found
- ❌ No responses to the Key Selection Criteria. For APS roles this is the decisive document — a strong resume without KSC responses will often not progress.
- ❌ The resume bullets restate the criteria as duties ("Managed multiple projects", "Communicated with stakeholders") with no evidence behind them.
- ❌ No quantified results anywhere. APS selection panels score evidence, not adjectives.
- ❌ "Results-driven" is filler. APS panels read past it; lead with a concrete claim instead.
- ❌ Seniority signalling is unclear. Make the scope (budget, stakeholders, timeframe) explicit so the panel can place you at the APS6 level.

### 4. Selection Criteria responses (STAR) — the core APS deliverable

**Criterion 1 — Demonstrated ability to manage competing priorities and deliver projects to deadline.**
- **Situation:** In 2024 I was running four service-improvement projects simultaneously, all due before the end of the financial year.
- **Task:** A key supplier slipped by three weeks, putting the largest project's deadline at risk while the other three still needed attention.
- **Action:** I re-sequenced the affected project's dependencies, moved non-blocked work forward, and ran a weekly prioritisation session with the project board so trade-offs were agreed openly rather than made in isolation. I kept a single shared tracker so all four projects stayed visible.
- **Result:** All four projects landed by the financial-year deadline; the largest came in two weeks early despite the supplier delay. The prioritisation session was adopted as standard practice by the team afterward.

**Criterion 2 — Strong communication and stakeholder engagement skills, including with senior executives.**
- **Situation:** A project I led affected three operational teams who initially had conflicting requirements, and it reported to an executive steering committee.
- **Task:** I needed to reconcile the teams into a single agreed scope and keep the executive committee confident enough to keep backing the project.
- **Action:** I ran scoping workshops with the three teams to surface and resolve the conflicts, then translated the outcome into fortnightly executive briefs written for a time-poor senior audience — short, risk-first, decision-ready.
- **Result:** The teams signed off a shared scope within three weeks, and the steering committee approved every stage gate on first presentation, citing the clarity of the briefs.

### 5. AU-specific notes
- This is a Commonwealth (APS) application — the **selection criteria responses are the main event**, not the cover letter. Lead with them.
- Address each criterion separately, in STAR, with one concrete example per criterion. Panels score against the criteria one by one.
- Keep dates as "Month YYYY"; AUD for any figures; Australian spelling (organise, program, prioritise).
- Match the language of the criteria back to the panel — if they say "stakeholder engagement", use that phrase, not a synonym.
- Make your APS level legible: state budget, number of stakeholders, and timeframes so the panel can map you to APS6.

### 6. Cover letter / application note
For APS roles a short pitch (often a "statement of claims" or one-page cover letter) sits alongside the criteria responses. Keep it to half a page: name the role and reference number, state in two or three sentences why you meet the criteria, and point the panel to your STAR responses for the evidence. Do not repeat the STAR content in full here.
