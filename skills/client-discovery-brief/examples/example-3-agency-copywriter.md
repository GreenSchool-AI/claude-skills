# Example 3 — Agency Hiring a Freelance Copywriter (subcontracting)

**Scenario (synthetic).** A digital agency messages a freelance copywriter: "Hey, we've got an overflow of work and a client project that needs a copywriter. Can you jump in? It's a website plus some emails. We'd white-label it under our brand. What's your day rate?"

---

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. Diagnostic questions (15-20) — ask before quoting
- What exactly is the deliverable — how many pages of web copy, how many emails, what word counts?
- Is there a brief and brand/tone guidelines from the end client, or am I creating the tone?
- Do I get access to the end client (calls, questions), or does everything route through you?
- Who owns the final IP, and is my work white-labelled (no attribution)?
- What's the deadline, and who controls the feedback turnaround — you or the end client?
- How many revision rounds, and who arbitrates "the client wants changes"?
- What are your payment terms — net 14, 30, 60? Paid on your invoice or only after the client pays you?
- Is this one project or likely ongoing overflow?
- Is there a non-solicit clause (can I work with this client directly later)?
- Who's my point of contact, and who signs off?
- Will I be briefed directly or via a brief written by someone who spoke to the client?
- Any existing copy to work from or match?

### 2. Request decomposed into deliverables
- In scope (pending answers): website copy for [N] pages + [N] marketing emails, to the agency's brief, white-labelled.
- Out of scope unless agreed: strategy/messaging workshops, SEO keyword research, additional pages or emails, unlimited revisions, direct client management.
- Success criteria: agency sign-off against the brief (not the end client's shifting opinion).
- Dependencies: agency supplies brief, brand guidelines, and consolidated feedback by [date].

### 3. Risks and red flags
- Feedback routed through the agency = the "telephone game"; the end client's changes arrive second-hand and can contradict the brief.
- "What's your day rate?" before scope is the same anchor-blind trap as a direct client.
- Agencies often pay on longer terms (net 30-60), and sometimes only "when the client pays us" — a cash-flow risk you didn't sign up for.
- White-label + no end-client access means you can't manage scope at the source.

### 4. Scope-creep risk areas
- "The client also wants…" changes passed through without re-scoping.
- Revision rounds multiplying because two parties (agency + client) both have opinions.
- Contract phrasing: "Includes the deliverables and [N] revision rounds defined above, against the agency brief. Changes originating from the end client after sign-off are treated as change orders billed at $[rate]."

### 5. Three pricing structures (AU context)
- Day rate — sensible while scope is fuzzy; common AU freelance copywriting range is ~$500-1,000 AUD/day depending on experience and niche. Agencies expect a trade/wholesale rate, but don't discount below your floor.
- Per-project fixed — only once deliverables and revision rounds are locked; protects you from the telephone-game churn.
- Retainer — if this is ongoing overflow, a monthly block of days at a slightly preferential rate, with a cap on volume.
GST: add 10% once over the $75k AUD threshold — agencies are GST-registered and expect a tax invoice.

### 6. Contract structure outline
- Payment terms in writing: net 14 or 30 on your invoice, **not** contingent on the agency being paid by their client.
- Defined deliverables and revision rounds against the agency brief; change-order process for end-client-driven changes.
- IP transfers to the agency on full payment (white-label); you retain the right to list the work type (if not the client name) unless a reasonable non-disclosure applies.
- Non-solicit: agree something fair and time-bounded, not a blanket lifetime ban on the client's industry.
- Kill fee if the project is cancelled after work starts.

### 7. Follow-up email draft
Hi [Name],
Happy to jump on this. Before I confirm a rate, can you send the brief and brand guidelines, and confirm the deliverables (page/email counts), the number of revision rounds, and your payment terms? My day rate is $[rate] + GST; for a defined scope I can also quote a fixed project price, which usually works better for both of us on white-label work. One thing to confirm: will feedback come consolidated from your side, or directly from the client?
Cheers, [Name]
