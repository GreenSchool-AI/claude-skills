# Example 2 — "My prices scare people off"

**Scenario (synthetic).** A boutique yoga studio in Melbourne says: "I offer one membership — $180 a month, unlimited classes. People do a trial, love it, then baulk at the price and don't sign up. Should I just drop the price?"

---

## How the skill responds

**Quick context check:** desired behaviour is *convert a happy trial-goer into a member*; the resistance is at the price, not the product (they love the classes). Dropping price is rarely the first answer — let's reframe how the price is presented before discounting the thing people already value.

**The models that fit here:**

### 1. Good-Better-Best (price relativity)
A single $180 option gives the brain nothing to compare to except $0. Add context.
**Do this:** Offer three tiers — e.g. Casual 4-pack ($80), Unlimited monthly ($180, "most popular"), and a Founding annual ($1,800/year = 2 months free). The middle now sits between a clear floor and a premium ceiling, and "most popular" nudges the default.

### 2. Anchoring
Whatever number people see first frames the rest.
**Do this:** Show the annual/premium figure first so $180/month reads as the sensible middle, not the top of the range.

### 3. Mental Accounting + Framing
$180/month is one big, scary "account". Re-slice it.
**Do this:** Present it as "about $6 a day — less than a coffee and a snack — for unlimited classes". Same money, friendlier mental account. Pair with a positive frame ("unlimited" not "$180").

### 4. Loss Aversion + Endowment (use the trial)
They've already felt ownership during the trial; leaving means losing something they have.
**Do this:** At trial's end: "Keep your spot and your progress — your unlimited access pauses on Friday." Frames signing up as avoiding a loss, not making a new purchase.

**What I'd try first (this month):**
1. Add the three-tier structure with the annual shown first and "most popular" on the monthly.
2. Reframe the monthly as a per-day cost on the join page.
3. Send a trial-end message built on loss aversion.
Only consider a real price drop if resistance persists after the framing changes — and even then, a time-boxed founding-member rate beats permanently cutting the price (which would anchor everyone low and signal lower quality via the rounded-price effect).

**Kept ethical:** the tiers and "most popular" label must be genuine, and the annual saving real.
