# Example 3 — Bad News to a Direct Report (Mode A)

**Scenario (synthetic).** A team lead at a mid-size logistics company has to tell a direct report that their role is affected by a restructure and a consultation process is starting. The draft tries to deliver the whole thing by email and over-softens it. Channel: email. This is a sensitive message, so the polish also flags *how* it should be delivered, not just the wording.

---

## INPUT

**Context:** Restructure confirmed by leadership; [Name]'s role is in scope for change/possible redundancy. Formal consultation hasn't started yet. Writing to a direct report the manager has worked with for three years.

**Draft (as supplied):**

> Hi [Name],
> I'm so sorry to have to tell you this over email, but there's going to be a restructure and unfortunately your role might be affected and possibly made redundant, I really don't know all the details yet but I wanted to give you a heads up. I feel terrible about it. We'll figure something out. Let's chat soon.
> So sorry again,

---

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. Quick assessment
First — this isn't a message to send as-is. Redundancy or role-change news shouldn't be broken in an email, and "we'll figure something out" is a promise you can't make during consultation. Use the email only to set up the conversation. The draft is 75 words and three apologies; the versions below are ~40, calm, and don't pre-empt the process.

### 2. Three versions (a meeting invite, not the news itself)

**Formal**
Subject: Meeting request — [date], [time]
Hi [Name],
I'd like to meet with you on [date] at [time] in [room/call] to talk through some upcoming changes to the team structure that affect your area. I want to discuss it with you directly rather than over email. Please let me know if that time works.
Kind regards, [Name]

**Neutral**
Hi [Name],
Can we meet [date] at [time]? There are some changes coming to the team structure that I want to talk through with you in person rather than put in an email. Let me know if that suits.
Cheers, [Name]

**Friendly (for a close, long-standing report — still measured)**
Hi [Name],
I'd like to grab some time with you [date] at [time] — there are some structural changes coming that affect your area, and I'd rather talk it through properly than email it. Does that time work?
Cheers, [Name]

### 3. Subject line options
- Direct: "Meeting request — [date], [time]"
- Neutral: "Time to talk [date]?"
- Action-required: "Can we meet [date] at [time]"

### 4. AU workplace culture notes
- Deliver this kind of news in a conversation, not in writing. The email's only job is to arrange that conversation respectfully and without blindsiding.
- Don't make commitments during a restructure ("we'll figure something out", "your job is safe"). Under a Fair Work consultation process, outcomes aren't yours to promise, and a written promise can be held against the business.
- Drop the apologies. Three "sorry"s read as panic and can imply a decision has already been made. Stay calm and steady — that's what reassures people.
- Keep the invite low-drama but not falsely casual; a too-breezy "quick chat" before bad news erodes trust afterwards.

### 5. What changed and why
The rewrite stops the email from doing the conversation's job. It arranges a respectful, in-person discussion, removes promises you can't keep, and cuts the apologies that would have made you look like you'd lost control of the situation. Prepare the actual message — and loop in HR on the consultation steps — before the meeting.
