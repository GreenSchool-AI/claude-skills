# Example 4 — Executive Status Update (Mode A)

**Scenario (synthetic).** A program manager at a financial-services firm sends a fortnightly update to the executive sponsor. The draft buries the one thing the executive needs to act on under a wall of detail. Channel: email.

---

## INPUT

**Context:** Fortnightly update to the executive sponsor (a GM). The project has slipped two weeks because a vendor missed a delivery, and the manager needs the GM to approve bringing in a contractor to recover the timeline.

**Draft (as supplied):**

> Hi [GM],
> Hope you're well! I wanted to give you an update on the project. The team has been working really hard over the last two weeks and we've made good progress on the integration workstream and the data migration is mostly on track. We did have a bit of an issue with the vendor who was a bit late delivering their component which has had some knock-on effects on the timeline. There were also a couple of smaller things with testing. Overall I think we might be looking at a delay of around two weeks. One option could be to bring in a contractor to help catch up but that would need budget approval. Let me know your thoughts when you get a chance!
> Cheers,

---

## OUTPUT (what the skill returns)

### 1. Quick assessment
Your draft is 130 words and the decision you need is in sentence eight. Executives scan — lead with status and the call you need from them. The version below is ~70 words, top line first.

### 2. Three versions

**Formal**
Subject: [Project] — 2-week slip, decision needed on recovery
Hi [GM],
Status: Amber. The project has slipped two weeks after a vendor missed a delivery.
Decision needed: approve a contractor (≈$[X]k AUD) to recover the timeline, or accept the two-week delay. I'd recommend the contractor.
Detail: integration and data migration are otherwise on track; testing has minor issues being managed.
Happy to talk it through. Can you confirm a direction by [date]?
Kind regards, [Name]

**Neutral**
Hi [GM],
Quick update — status is Amber. A late vendor delivery has pushed us two weeks.
I need a call from you: bring in a contractor (≈$[X]k AUD) to claw the time back, or accept the delay. My recommendation is the contractor.
Everything else (integration, data migration) is on track. Can you confirm by [date]?
Cheers, [Name]

**Friendly (sponsor you have rapport with)**
Hi [GM],
Status: Amber. We've lost two weeks to a late vendor delivery.
The decision I need from you: approve a contractor (≈$[X]k AUD) to recover, or we take the two-week hit. I'd go contractor. Rest of the project's tracking fine.
Can you give me a steer by [date]?
Cheers, [Name]

### 3. Subject line options
- Direct: "[Project] — 2-week slip, decision needed on recovery"
- Curiosity: "[Project] — quick call I need from you"
- Action-required: "[Project] — budget approval needed by [date]"

### 4. AU workplace culture notes
- Lead with a RAG status (Red/Amber/Green) and the decision needed. Executives triage their inbox by what requires them to act.
- "Hope you're well!" and "when you get a chance" signal low urgency on a message that needs a dated response. Replace with a clear "by [date]".
- Put your recommendation on the table. Australian executives generally prefer a manager who proposes a call over one who just surfaces a problem.
- Detail goes last, in one line — the GM can ask for more if they want it.

### 5. What changed and why
The rewrite inverts the draft: status and decision first, supporting detail compressed to a single line. The ask is now explicit, dated, and carries a recommendation, so the GM can act in ten seconds instead of mining the paragraph for what you actually need.
